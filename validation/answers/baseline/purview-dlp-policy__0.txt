Building a Microsoft Purview DLP policy to stop sensitive data leaving via email and endpoints is done in the Microsoft Purview compliance portal. Here is how I would approach it.

1. Decide what you are protecting
- Identify the sensitive data: e.g. credit card numbers, national IDs, bank details, health data, or your own confidential business data.
- Map this to sensitive information types (SITs) - Purview has many built-in (credit card, passport, SSN, etc.) and you can build custom SITs (keywords, regex, dictionaries) or use exact data match (EDM) for precise matching against your own data. You can also key off sensitivity labels and trainable classifiers.

2. Choose the locations (workloads)
- For this scenario, target:
  - Exchange Online (email).
  - Devices (endpoint DLP) - covers actions like copy to USB, upload to web/cloud, copy to clipboard, print, paste.
- You can also include SharePoint, OneDrive, Teams, and cloud apps (via Defender for Cloud Apps) later for full coverage.
- Endpoint DLP prerequisites: onboard the devices to Purview/Defender, and ensure supported OS/browser (use the Purview extension or a supported browser for web upload controls).

3. Design the policy and rules
- Create a DLP policy and add rules with conditions and actions.
- Conditions: content contains the chosen SITs, at or above a confidence level and instance count (e.g. "credit card, high confidence, 1 or more" or "10 or more" to target bulk).
- Actions for email: block the message, allow with override + business justification, encrypt, or notify.
- Actions for endpoints: block or audit the egress activities (USB, cloud upload, print, clipboard, network share).
- Distinguish recipients/scope: e.g. block sending to external recipients but only audit internal, and restrict to/from cloud egress on endpoints.

4. Add user education and overrides
- Configure policy tips so users see why an action was blocked, in Outlook and on endpoints.
- Decide whether to allow user override with justification (good for early rollout and reducing friction) versus hard block for the most sensitive data.
- Send incident reports/alerts to compliance/security admins.

5. Roll out safely - test mode first
- Start every policy in Test/simulation mode (test without notifications, then test with policy tips). This shows what would be blocked without disrupting users.
- Review the DLP alerts and activity explorer to see matches, volume, and false positives.
- Tune SIT confidence levels, instance counts, and exceptions to cut false positives.
- Then turn the policy On (enforce), ideally to a pilot group first, before org-wide.

6. Monitor and operate
- Use Activity Explorer, the DLP alerts dashboard, and reports to monitor matches and incidents.
- Investigate and tune regularly.
- Integrate alerts with Sentinel/Defender if you want them in the SOC queue.

Summary flow: pick the sensitive info types -> scope to Exchange + Devices -> write rules with conditions (SIT + confidence + count) and actions (block/encrypt/audit + policy tips with optional override) -> run in test/simulation mode -> tune for false positives -> enforce to a pilot then broadly -> monitor in Activity Explorer. Starting in simulation and tuning before enforcing is what keeps it from disrupting the business.
