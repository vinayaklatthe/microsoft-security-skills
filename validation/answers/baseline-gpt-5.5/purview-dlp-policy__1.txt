To reduce false positives for credit card numbers, tune the rule so it looks for real payment card data in the right context, not any 16-digit number.

1. Review current matches
- Use Activity explorer, DLP alerts, and matched event details.
- Look at the content type, location, sender, recipient, confidence level, instance count, and surrounding text.
- Separate true card data from order numbers, tracking numbers, employee IDs, invoice numbers, and test data.

2. Increase confidence
- Use the credit card sensitive information type at higher confidence.
- Require supporting evidence such as keywords near the number, for example card, expiry, CVV, Visa, Mastercard, payment, or billing.
- Require a higher instance count for lower-confidence matches.

3. Add exceptions
- Exclude known business identifiers that match card-like patterns.
- Exclude approved templates, internal test data, or trusted senders where appropriate.
- Exclude approved domains or systems only when the data flow is expected and documented.

4. Tune by location
- Use different thresholds for Exchange, SharePoint, OneDrive, Teams, and endpoints.
- Email to external recipients may need stricter controls than internal SharePoint storage.
- Endpoint copy to USB may need stricter controls than viewing a document.

5. Use sensitivity labels where possible
- Combine sensitive information types with labels such as Confidential or Highly Confidential.
- Apply stronger restrictions when card data appears in labelled sensitive documents.

6. Use policy tips and overrides during tuning
- Let users submit business justification during pilot.
- Review override reasons to identify legitimate workflows and noisy rules.
- Move to blocking only when precision is acceptable.

7. Test before enforcement
- Build a test set with real examples of true positives and false positives.
- Run the policy in test mode.
- Adjust confidence, instance count, keywords, and exceptions until alerts are useful.

The key is context. A DLP rule that matches a card number plus payment context is much more accurate than a rule that matches any long number.
