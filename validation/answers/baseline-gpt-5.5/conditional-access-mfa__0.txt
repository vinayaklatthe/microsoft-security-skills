Design the Conditional Access baseline in stages so you improve security without locking out users or administrators.

1. Prepare emergency access
- Create at least two cloud-only emergency access accounts.
- Exclude them from Conditional Access policies that could block all access.
- Monitor and alert on any sign-in from these accounts.

2. Inventory users, apps, and risk
- Identify administrators, standard users, guests, service accounts, break-glass accounts, legacy protocols, critical apps, and locations.
- Identify apps that cannot support modern authentication.

3. Start in report-only mode
- Build policies in report-only mode first.
- Review impact logs to see who would be blocked or prompted.
- Fix service accounts, legacy auth, and app compatibility issues before enforcing.

4. Baseline policies
- Require MFA for all administrators.
- Require MFA for all users, at least when risk, location, or application sensitivity requires it.
- Block legacy authentication.
- Require compliant or hybrid joined devices for sensitive apps where appropriate.
- Require phishing-resistant MFA for privileged roles.
- Use sign-in risk and user risk policies if licensed.

5. Use groups and rollout rings
- Pilot with IT and a small business group.
- Expand to departments or regions.
- Keep exclusions temporary and reviewed.
- Avoid excluding broad groups such as all admins or all executives.

6. Tune session and access controls
- Use sign-in frequency and persistent browser session controls where needed.
- Apply stricter controls to admin portals and sensitive apps.
- Use named locations carefully. Do not treat office IPs as fully trusted without other controls.

7. Monitor enforcement
- Review Conditional Access insights, sign-in logs, failures, and user helpdesk tickets.
- Alert on policy changes and emergency account use.
- Document every policy purpose, owner, included users, excluded users, and rollback plan.

8. Move to enforcement
- Enable policies ring by ring.
- Keep a tested rollback path.
- Review policies quarterly and after major application or identity changes.

The safest rollout is report-only, pilot, measure, fix impact, enforce, then expand.
